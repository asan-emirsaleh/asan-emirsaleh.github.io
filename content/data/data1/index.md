## Overview

This dataset contains a curated collection list of short audio recordings illustrating non-standard phonological phenomena in Crimean Tatar dialects from the Sudak (Sudaq) region, Crimea. The recordings were conducted by Asan Emirsale, Girey Bair, Refat Izzet, Enver Mecit, during 2009-2026. Recordings deposited on video hosting platforms were also incorporated. The recordings are annotated with IPA transcription.

#### Terminal consonant aspiration
The presence of terminal aspirated phonemes argues for a very deep and systemic nature of aspiration phenomena in dialectal language of the Sudaq region. In a language without systemic aspiration — where aspirated and non-aspirated correlates do not contrast with each other to form semantic distinctions — aspiration tends to be lost in weak positions, such as at the end of a word. However, in Sudaq speach, the terminal aspiration is typically preserved.

### Glottalization
Ejective consonants, also referred to as explosives, are characterized by a sharp glottal clousure that is absent in standard Crimean Tatar as well as in most Turkic languages. The full range of phonemes documented includes both palatalized and regular correlates.

### Retroflex consonants
In the local language spoken in the Sudaq, noisy and retroflex allophones of the \[ʃ\] are common. These also may occur in aspirated, labialized or compound aspirated-labialized forms. It is very natural for the \[ʂ\] to tend toward \[f\]. The retroflex affricate \[ꭧ\] is rare in Sudaq dialectal language nowdays, but is still present in both regular and palatalized forms.

### Ejective-like sibilants
There is another sibilant that is very characteristic of Sudaq phonetics, preiminarily classified as an ejective fricative \[sʼ\], but one that also represents some phonetic effects of the retroflex fricative \[ʂ\], and still requires proper classification. There is also a voiced correlate of \[sʼ\] tending to affricate, which we code as \[zʼ\]. This is a common allophone of \[z\], and sometimes transforms into a distinguishable affricate. It is natural for \[zʼ\] to partially lose voicing.

### Fortis and consonant doubling
The increased strength and length of certain consonants is very common in the local language spoken in Sudaq. It is usually combined with ejection, but not always obviously so. Besides the very natural ejection and fortis articulation at the very first consonant of the phrasal syntagma, the fortis articulation may also occur in the middle of a word.

### Glottal frikatives
In local language of Sudaq region, the glottal fricatives, both voiced and voiceless glottal fricatives are present, along with their pharyngeal allophones. Speakers naturally alternate between the glottal and pharyngeal forms, with preferring the faryngeal form when directly asked to identify the exact sound to be pronounced.

## Source of data
The dataset is constructed using both original field recordings made by compilers and publicly available recordings such as those deposited on video hosting platforms or radio archives.

Field recordings were conducted between 2008 and 2025 across Crimea where repatriated people of Sudaq origin are settled. The speakers represent communities from Büyük Taraqtaş and Küçük Taraqtaş, Xutlax, Qoz and Xapsxor, reflecting the oghuz-type language spoken in the Sudaq region and covering both eastern and western branches of the Oghuz-type dialect of the Sudaq region.

Publicly available recordings were sourced from YouTube, including material published by Qaradeñiz Production and "Qırım Akademiyası" initiative.
