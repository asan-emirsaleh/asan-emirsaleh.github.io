## Impressions

I recently visited the annual exhibition of the Society of Artists of Botanical Art (SABA). Despite the development of instrument-mediated methods for capturing objects on a two-dimensional plane — analogue and now digital photography, available in every mobile phone — as well as the emergence of specialised generative models, botanical art continues to exist. This is most heartening. I knew that the school of botanical illustration still exists in many European countries, in the United States, and in the Commonwealth nations. But that an active — not a relic, but a living, functioning — community of practitioners exists right beside me, I did not know and had never suspected.

I have a particular interest in this branch of art. On the one hand, I am a botanist by training. And morphological characters of plants remain the principal means of knowing them, despite the acknowledged limitations and instability of such characters. Now, as a hundred, two hundred, and three hundred years ago, a botanical description of a new plant taxon meticulously details its external characters. The ability to perceive barely discernible, hard-to-classify external features of plants constitutes the very core of expert competence for botanists and plant collectors alike. Therefore, to see and to be able to convey precisely what distinguishes one species from another, outwardly very similar one, is of great value.

Another reason why botanical art occupies a place of exceptional importance in my worldview lies in my commitment to analogue technologies. A professional, competently executed illustration is a document. Not as absolute as a herbarium specimen, but standing on a par with analogue photographic film. It is a genuine material carrier of information.

And there is yet another reason. Painting, for me, is one of the ways of knowing the world. It is not a skill (and indeed, my technical level in painting is modest, and in drawing quite poor) — it is a path, a mode of perceiving reality. From time to time I take up a pastel stick or a brush. Most often I go out to work en plein air, but occasionally I work from a photograph. This is how, for instance, this pastel of a *Cichorium intybus* inflorescence came about.

The exhibition struck me on multiple counts at once. First, the number of artists whose works are exhibited at the Museum of Nature. These are all works from 2025, not archival pieces. Second, the sheer quantity of these works.

I was particularly impressed by the cuticular layer on the surface of the blueberry fruits.

And the final impression — I was astounded by the completeness with which the biological nature of the subjects is rendered. A single image brings together features and developmental stages that are nearly impossible to capture simultaneously in a photograph. Phytopathological features are also conveyed with striking realism and in a way that reflects their essence. It is here that the botanical expertise of the artists reveals itself.

## Thoughts

Lately I have been deeply sympathising with artists over the uncertain future of the craft of botanical illustration. High-quality scientific illustrations are genuinely in demand and will remain so for a very long time. The problem lies in the publishing industry and in the conscientiousness of book authors. The advent of the Internet has led to the accumulation of a vast amount of multimedia material available everywhere. Most of these materials are distributed under Creative Commons licences, but their repeated reuse has severed the link to information about the original licence and authorship. Whereas previously a book author had no way of sourcing illustrations other than obtaining them from a photographer or an artist, now images from the Internet are increasingly used as illustrations. Naturally, in the published books they appear without attribution to the original authors, thereby violating the terms of the licence.

This applies to a greater extent to children's literature. Many interesting children's educational books are now appearing in which photographs are used instead of drawings. It is hard to agree that the choice of photography makes a book more colourful, more vivid, or easier to understand, but it unambiguously makes it cheaper. The authors — among whom are many of my acquaintances whom I deeply respect — have compromised with their conscience for the sake of simplifying their routine, shortening the publication cycle, and reaping the dividends solely for themselves.

Here I am compelled to narrow the context to the realities of Crimean publishing. Children's literature is perhaps the only type of book in the Crimean Tatar language that sells well. Obviously, such books are half made up of illustrations. And it is precisely here that the authors — innovators whom I respect, distinguished educators, or popular essayists — take the path of least resistance. How ethical it is to use other people's photographs as illustrations without citing the original source is a separate question (clearly, it is unethical). What is alarming is something else — that this has become an accepted standard practice.

Is the close-knit community of Crimean educators, authors, and publishers working in the Crimean Tatar language ready to discuss these questions? I believe their readiness is beside the point. They have committed an ethical transgression and must face the consequences. The ultimate aim of this dialogue should be the development of high standards of ethics and publishing culture, or at the very least the adoption of those rules that already exist in established professional communities (for instance, in European academic circles).

Separately, another episode deserves mention — the production of souvenirs featuring historical photographs of Crimea. A group of young designers produced commemorative magnets with monochrome images of Crimean architectural monuments. The problem, however, is that these images have a rights holder who is most likely entirely unaware of such commercial use of photographs he purchased at auction and shared free of charge on social media under a Creative Commons licence. After I pointed out to the authors of this initiative that such use of other people's images may entail legal (and, naturally, ethical) complications, I was banned from their Telegram channel, despite my personal acquaintance with the channel's authors and the complete absence of any conflict whatsoever, including personal conflict or conflict of interest.

"We are those whose standards we do follow".
